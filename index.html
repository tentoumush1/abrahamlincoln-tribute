About the 16th
President
of United States

Early Life
(1809-1830)

Abraham Lincoln was born on February 12, 1809, in a log cabin in Kentucky. Raised in poverty, he had little formal education and was mostly self-taught. In 1816, his family moved to Indiana, where he developed a strong work ethic while reading and learning independently.

Career & Politics
(1831-1860)

After moving to Illinois in 1830, Lincoln worked as a store clerk before entering politics. In 1834, he was elected to the Illinois House of Representatives, serving four terms. He later became a lawyer and, in 1846, was elected to the U.S. Congress for a single term.

Presidency
(1861-1865)

Elected as the 16th U.S. President in 1860, Lincoln led the nation through the Civil War. He issued the Emancipation Proclamation in 1863 and helped pass the Thirteenth Amendment, abolishing slavery.

Assassination
(1865)

On April 14, 1865, Lincoln was shot by John Wilkes Booth at Ford’s Theatre and died the next morning. His assassination marked a tragic end to his leadership, just days after the Civil War had concluded.

Achivements and Contributions

Abolition of Slavery

The Emancipation Proclamation, issued by Lincoln in 1863, declared the freedom of all slaves in Confederate-held territory, transforming the Civil War into a fight for human freedom.

Role in the American Civil War

Lincoln played a crucial role in preserving the Union during the Civil War, leading the North in defeating the Confederate states and ending slavery.

Gettysburg Address and Its Impact

In his brief yet powerful Gettysburg Address in 1863, Lincoln redefined the Civil War as a struggle for equality and freedom, influencing the nation's values for generations.

13th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution

Lincoln supported and helped ensure the passage of the Thirteenth Amendment in 1865, which abolished slavery throughout the United States.
